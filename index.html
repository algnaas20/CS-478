Essay 1Define computer graphics and give three applications.Midterm

Computer graphics is the study of using computers to create and interact with visuals. Applications include user interfaces, digital maps, fonts, games, CAD, simulation, and robotics visualization.

Essay 2Compare modeling, rendering, and animation.Midterm

Modeling creates computational geometry, rendering generates images from that geometry, and animation represents motion and change over time.

Essay 3Explain raster images, resolution, depth, and framebuffer.Midterm

A raster image is a grid of pixels. Resolution is the grid width and height. Depth is pixel precision. The framebuffer is memory that stores pixel values.

Essay 4Compare LCD and OLED displays.Midterm

LCD modulates a backlight using liquid crystals and polarizers. OLED pixels emit their own light, so they can turn off individual pixels and often produce deeper blacks.

Essay 5Explain flicker fusion rate and why frame rate matters.Midterm

Flicker fusion rate is the frequency where flickering appears steady to humans. Higher frame rates reduce visible flicker and motion discontinuity, depending on lighting and field of view.

Essay 6Describe rasterization in one paragraph.Midterm

Rasterization converts geometric primitives such as lines and triangles into fragments or pixels. It is fast and fits GPU pipelines, but it needs visibility handling such as Z-buffering.

Essay 7Explain the Z-buffer algorithm.Midterm

For each pixel, the Z-buffer stores the closest depth so far. A new fragment updates the color only if its depth passes the test, solving visibility efficiently.

Essay 8Explain barycentric interpolation.Midterm

Barycentric coordinates express a point inside a triangle as weights of the triangle vertices. They are used to interpolate colors, normals, depth, and texture coordinates.

Essay 9Why can painter's algorithm fail?Midterm

Painter's algorithm sorts geometry back-to-front, but one global order can fail when triangles overlap cyclically or intersect. Z-buffering handles per-pixel visibility better.

Essay 10Explain ray casting and ray tracing.Midterm

Ray casting shoots rays from the camera through pixels to find the closest visible object. Ray tracing extends that idea recursively with reflection, refraction, and shadow rays.

Essay 11Why do we need spatial acceleration structures?Module 6

Naive ray tracing tests many rays against many objects. Acceleration structures reduce the number of expensive intersection tests by organizing space or objects.

Essay 12Compare uniform grids, BVHs, and k-d trees.Module 6

Uniform grids split space regularly, BVHs group objects in bounding volumes, and k-d trees split space adaptively. Each has different build, memory, and traversal tradeoffs.

Essay 13Explain bounding volumes and the tightness tradeoff.Module 6

A bounding volume is a simple shape around a complex object. Tighter volumes reject more rays but may be harder to intersect; simpler volumes are cheaper but less precise.

Essay 14Explain SAH in BVH construction.Module 6

The Surface Area Heuristic estimates expected ray traversal cost using surface areas and primitive counts, then chooses splits that reduce expected intersection work.

Essay 15What is a shading model?Module 7

A shading model approximates how light interacts with a surface. It uses light direction, surface normal, material properties, and sometimes view direction to compute color.

Essay 16Compare Lambertian and Phong/Blinn-Phong shading.Module 7

Lambertian shading models diffuse matte reflection. Phong and Blinn-Phong add specular highlights, making shiny surfaces depend on the viewer and light directions.

Essay 17Compare flat, Gouraud, and Phong shading.Module 7

Flat shading evaluates once per face. Gouraud shades vertices and interpolates colors. Phong interpolates normals and shades per fragment, producing smoother highlights.

Essay 18Compare implicit and explicit geometry.Module 8

Implicit geometry defines surfaces with equations or functions, useful for inside/outside tests and CSG. Explicit geometry stores surface elements directly, such as polygon meshes.

Essay 19Explain signed distance functions.Module 8

A signed distance function returns distance to the nearest surface plus a sign indicating inside or outside. It is useful for implicit modeling and distance-field rendering.

Essay 20What is CSG?Module 8

Constructive Solid Geometry builds complex shapes from simpler primitives using Boolean operations such as union, intersection, and difference.

Essay 21Why are triangle meshes popular?Module 9

Triangle meshes are simple, flexible, GPU-friendly, and can approximate many surfaces. Triangles are planar and easy for rasterization pipelines.

Essay 22Compare triangle soup and vertex-face mesh representation.Module 9

Triangle soup stores independent triangles and is simple but lacks connectivity. Vertex-face lists reuse vertices and better preserve mesh structure.

Essay 23Why do we use homogeneous coordinates?Module 10

Homogeneous coordinates add an extra coordinate so translations, rotations, scales, and projections can be represented and composed with matrices.

Essay 24Explain model, view, and projection transformations.Module 10

Model moves an object from local to world space. View moves the world into the camera frame. Projection maps camera space into clip/NDC space.

Essay 25Why does transformation order matter?Module 10

Matrix multiplication is not commutative. Rotating then translating can produce a different result from translating then rotating, so the intended order must be preserved.

Essay 26What is texture mapping?Module 11

Texture mapping associates data from a texture image with points on a surface, allowing detailed appearance without adding geometric complexity.

Essay 27Compare sphere mapping and cube mapping.Module 11

Sphere mapping stores the environment in one image but suffers distortion and singularities. Cube mapping uses six faces, reducing distortion but using more storage.

Essay 28Explain shadow mapping.Module 11

Shadow mapping renders the scene from the light's point of view and stores depth. From the camera view, points are compared to that map to decide whether they are shadowed.

Essay 29Why are mipmaps useful?Module 11

Mipmaps store prefiltered lower-resolution texture levels. When a texture is minified, selecting an appropriate mip level reduces aliasing and shimmering.

Essay 30Compare keyframing, motion capture, and simulation.Module 12

Keyframing is artist-directed interpolation, motion capture is data-driven from sensors, and simulation is physics-based motion computed from models.

Essay 31What is tweening?Module 12

Tweening is interpolation between keyframes. The animator defines important states, and the system fills in the in-between motion.

Essay 32What are strengths and weaknesses of motion capture?Module 12

Motion capture creates realistic complex motion from real performances, but it requires sensors and captured motion, and editing the result can be difficult.

Essay 33Why are GPUs described as massively parallel linear algebra machines?Module 13

Graphics workloads contain many similar operations on vertices and fragments, and most of these operations are vector and matrix calculations that run well in parallel.

Essay 34Explain the programmable graphics pipeline.Module 13

The application sends scene data, the vertex shader processes vertices, primitives are assembled and rasterized, and the fragment shader computes per-fragment output for the framebuffer.

Essay 35Compare uniforms and varyings in shaders.Module 13

Uniforms are read-only global values shared across shader invocations. Varyings pass per-vertex data that gets interpolated and received by fragment shaders.
